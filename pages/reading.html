1. Section Overview

The Reading Comprehension section of the IBEW aptitude test contains 36 questions to be completed in 51 minutes — giving you roughly 85 seconds per question. The test presents several passages, each followed by a set of multiple-choice questions that assess how well you understand, interpret, and analyze written material.

⚠ Important: The passages are not necessarily electrical in nature. You will encounter topics from science, history, culture, technology, and the humanities. The test measures your general reading ability — not your prior knowledge of any subject.

The questions evaluate your ability to:

  • Identify the main idea or primary purpose of a passage
  • Retrieve and interpret specific details stated in the text
  • Draw logical inferences from information presented
  • Determine the meaning of vocabulary in context
  • Recognize the author's tone, attitude, or perspective
  • Distinguish between facts stated and opinions implied

The reading score is combined with your math score to produce a single composite result. Most JATC programs require a minimum score of 4 out of 9 or higher, depending on local competitiveness.

2. Reading Strategies

Effective reading comprehension on a timed test requires more than just "reading carefully." Use these proven strategies to maximize accuracy and speed.

Strategy 1: Read the Questions First

Before diving into a passage, scan the questions briefly. Knowing what you're looking for primes your brain to spot relevant details as you read. This approach works especially well for "specific detail retrieval" and "vocabulary in context" questions. If time is tight, skip the passage-first approach entirely.

Strategy 2: Passage-First (Deep Comprehension)

For "main idea" and "author's tone" questions, reading the passage thoroughly before looking at the questions often yields better accuracy. Read the first and last paragraph carefully — authors tend to state their thesis and conclusion in these sections. Skim the body paragraphs for structure, paying attention to topic sentences.

Strategy 3: Identify the Main Idea

After reading, ask yourself: "What is the single most important point the author is making?" If you can articulate this in one sentence, you've found the main idea. Be wary of answers that are too broad (they go beyond the passage) or too narrow (they focus on a minor detail).

Strategy 4: Scan for Details

When a question asks about a specific fact, statistic, name, or event, don't re-read the entire passage. Instead, use keywords from the question to scan the text — your eye should hunt for those words or synonyms. Once located, read the surrounding sentence or two for context.

Strategy 5: Use Context Clues

When you encounter an unfamiliar word, look at the words and sentences around it. Often, the author provides a definition, a restatement, an example, or a contrast that reveals the word's meaning. Signal phrases like "that is," "in other words," or "such as" are especially helpful clues.

Strategy 6: Eliminate Wrong Answers

For every question, try to eliminate at least two answer choices before selecting your final answer. Common wrong-answer patterns include: (a) statements that contradict the passage, (b) statements that are true but not mentioned in the text, (c) extreme language like "always" or "never" that the author didn't use, and (d) answers that are partially correct but mix in an incorrect element.

3. Question Types

Every question on the IBEW reading test falls into one of five categories. Recognizing the question type tells you how to approach it.

1. Main Idea / Primary Purpose

These questions ask what the passage as a whole is about, or why the author wrote it. Keywords: "main idea," "primary purpose," "title," "mainly concerned with."

Strategy: Focus on the opening and closing paragraphs. Eliminate answers that are too narrow (one detail) or too broad (extends beyond the passage). The correct answer captures the passage's overall scope.

2. Specific Detail Retrieval

These questions ask you to locate and confirm a fact explicitly stated in the passage. Keywords: "according to the passage," "the passage states," "the author mentions."

Strategy: Use keyword scanning. Identify a unique word or phrase in the question, locate it in the passage, and verify that the answer choice matches the text exactly. Do not rely on memory alone — confirm by re-reading that section.

3. Inference / Conclusion

These questions ask you to draw a logical conclusion based on information that is implied but not directly stated. Keywords: "it can be inferred," "the passage suggests," "most likely."

Strategy: Infer only what must be true based on the passage. Eliminate answers that go beyond reasonable inference or require outside knowledge. The correct inference is fully supported by the text even though it's not explicitly stated.

4. Vocabulary in Context

These questions ask for the meaning of a word or phrase as it is used in the passage. Keywords: "closest in meaning to," "as used in the passage," "the word [X] most nearly means."

Strategy: Go back to the sentence containing the word and read the surrounding sentences. Predict the meaning before looking at the answer choices. The correct answer is how the author uses the word — not necessarily its most common dictionary definition.

5. Author's Tone / Attitude

These questions ask about the author's feelings or perspective toward the subject. Keywords: "tone," "attitude," "the author would most likely agree."

Strategy: Pay attention to the author's word choice — adjectives, adverbs, and emotionally charged language reveal tone. Look for words like "remarkably," "unfortunately," or "promising." Tone descriptions like "cautiously optimistic," "skeptical," or "enthusiastic" are common correct answers.

4. Passage A — Renewable Energy

The Shifting Landscape of Renewable Energy

Over the past two decades, renewable energy has moved from the fringes of the global energy conversation to its center. Solar and wind power, once dismissed as expensive and unreliable alternatives, have undergone dramatic cost reductions. According to the International Renewable Energy Agency, the cost of utility-scale solar photovoltaic electricity fell by 82% between 2010 and 2020. Onshore wind costs dropped by approximately 40% over the same period. These declines have made renewables the cheapest source of new electricity generation in many parts of the world, even without government subsidies.

However, the rapid expansion of renewable energy has not been without obstacles. One significant challenge is intermittency: the sun does not always shine, and the wind does not always blow. This variability requires grid operators to maintain backup power sources, often natural gas plants, which can be ramped up and down quickly. Advances in battery storage technology are beginning to address this problem, but large-scale, cost-effective storage remains an active area of research and development.

Another barrier involves the physical infrastructure required for a renewable-heavy grid. Transmission lines must be extended to connect remote solar farms and wind installations to population centers. In the United States alone, some estimates suggest that achieving a fully decarbonized grid would require thousands of miles of new high-voltage transmission lines. Permitting and land-use conflicts often delay these projects for years.

Despite these hurdles, momentum continues to build. Corporate demand for clean energy has surged, with hundreds of multinational companies pledging to source 100% of their electricity from renewables. Governments worldwide have set ambitious targets: the European Union aims to derive 32% of its energy from renewable sources by 2030, and China, the world's largest emitter, has pledged to reach peak carbon emissions before 2030 and achieve carbon neutrality by 2060.

The transition to renewable energy is not merely a technological shift — it is an economic and social transformation. Communities that once depended on coal mining or oil drilling are grappling with job displacement, while new industries around solar installation, wind turbine maintenance, and battery manufacturing are emerging. Policymakers face the delicate task of balancing environmental goals with economic stability for affected workers and regions. Ultimately, the question is no longer whether the world will transition to renewable energy, but how quickly and equitably that transition will occur.

Adapted from energy policy reports and industry analysis.

Question A1 — Main Idea
Which of the following best expresses the main idea of the passage?
Correct Answer: B. The passage covers both the progress (falling costs, corporate demand, government targets) and the challenges (intermittency, transmission infrastructure, community impacts). Option A is too narrow — other renewables aren't discussed. Option C captures only one detail. Option D directly contradicts the passage, which states storage "remains an active area of research."
Question A2 — Specific Detail Retrieval
According to the passage, by what percentage did utility-scale solar photovoltaic electricity costs fall between 2010 and 2020?
Correct Answer: C. The first paragraph explicitly states: "the cost of utility-scale solar photovoltaic electricity fell by 82% between 2010 and 2020." The 40% figure refers to onshore wind costs, not solar.
Question A3 — Vocabulary in Context
As used in the second paragraph, the word "intermittency" most nearly means:
Correct Answer: C. The passage explains intermittency with a colon: "the sun does not always shine, and the wind does not always blow." This directly describes variable or unpredictable availability — not cost, government policy, or transport issues.
Question A4 — Inference
The passage suggests that achieving a fully decarbonized grid in the United States would most likely:
Correct Answer: B. The third paragraph states that "achieving a fully decarbonized grid would require thousands of miles of new high-voltage transmission lines." This supports B. The passage does not say it's impossible (A), does not say storage is the sole factor (C), and makes no claim about timing relative to EU targets (D).
Question A5 — Author's Tone
The author's overall tone toward the renewable energy transition can best be described as:
Correct Answer: C. The author presents substantial progress ("momentum continues to build," "surged") while also devoting considerable space to obstacles. The final sentence — "how quickly and equitably" — frames the transition as inevitable but with ongoing challenges, which matches "cautiously optimistic."

5. Passage B — History of Communication

From Smoke Signals to Fiber Optics: The Evolution of Long-Distance Communication

Human beings have always sought ways to communicate across distance. For millennia, this meant physical messengers carrying spoken or written words on foot, by horse, or by ship — a process measured in days, weeks, or even months. The earliest known long-distance signaling systems, such as smoke signals used by Indigenous peoples of the Americas and beacon fires lit across ancient China's Great Wall, could convey simple prearranged messages far faster than any human runner. Yet these systems were limited to a handful of predetermined meanings.

The first true breakthrough in flexible long-distance communication came in the late eighteenth century with Claude Chappe's semaphore telegraph in France. Chappe's system placed towers topped with movable wooden arms every ten to fifteen kilometers. Operators relayed messages by positioning the arms into specific configurations representing letters and numbers. In clear weather, a message could travel from Paris to the French border — over 800 kilometers — in less than an hour. By the 1840s, France had built over 5,000 kilometers of semaphore lines.

The semaphore's dominance was short-lived. In 1844, Samuel Morse sent the famous message "What hath God wrought" from Washington, D.C., to Baltimore, demonstrating the electric telegraph. Unlike semaphores, the electric telegraph worked at night, in fog, and through rain. Within two decades, telegraph cables crisscrossed continents and, in 1866, the first permanent transatlantic cable connected Europe and North America. Messages that once took weeks by ship now arrived in minutes.

The telephone, patented by Alexander Graham Bell in 1876, added the human voice to the electric revolution. By the turn of the twentieth century, millions of telephones connected homes and businesses. Radio communication soon followed, pioneered by Guglielmo Marconi, who transmitted the first wireless signal across the Atlantic in 1901. Radio freed communication from physical wires entirely, enabling ship-to-shore contact and, eventually, mass broadcasting that would reshape global culture.

Today, fiber optic cables carrying pulses of light form the backbone of the internet, transmitting data at speeds that would have seemed miraculous to Morse or Bell. A single fiber optic strand can carry more information in one second than the entire telegraph network of the 1860s could carry in a year. Yet the fundamental human drive remains unchanged from the days of smoke signals: the desire to shrink the distance between people, to make the world a smaller and more connected place.

Adapted from historical accounts of communication technology.

6. Passage C — Nutrition & Sleep

The Overlooked Connection Between Diet and Sleep Quality

Most people understand that what they eat affects their weight, energy levels, and long-term health. Far fewer recognize that diet also exerts a powerful influence on the quality and duration of their sleep. In recent years, researchers have uncovered a bidirectional relationship between nutrition and sleep: poor dietary choices can impair sleep, and insufficient sleep can, in turn, drive unhealthy eating patterns.

Studies using large population datasets have found consistent associations between certain dietary patterns and sleep outcomes. Diets high in saturated fats and refined sugars are linked to lighter, more fragmented sleep, with more frequent nighttime awakenings. Conversely, diets rich in fiber, whole grains, fruits, and vegetables are associated with deeper, more restorative slow-wave sleep. One analysis published in the Journal of Clinical Sleep Medicine found that participants who consumed a high-fiber, low-saturated-fat diet fell asleep faster — averaging a 17-minute reduction in the time it took to drift off — compared to those eating a diet heavy in processed foods and added sugars.

The mechanisms behind these effects are still being unraveled, but several pathways have been identified. Tryptophan, an amino acid found in turkey, eggs, dairy products, and nuts, serves as a precursor to serotonin and melatonin, the neurotransmitters that regulate the sleep-wake cycle. Magnesium and calcium, abundant in leafy greens and dairy, help muscles relax and support the production of gamma-aminobutyric acid, a neurotransmitter that quiets brain activity before sleep. High sugar intake, by contrast, can cause blood glucose spikes followed by sharp drops, triggering the release of cortisol and adrenaline — hormones that promote alertness at precisely the wrong time.

The reverse direction of this relationship is equally important. Sleep deprivation alters the balance of two key hormones that govern appetite: ghrelin, which signals hunger, and leptin, which signals satiety. When a person sleeps fewer than six hours per night, ghrelin levels rise and leptin levels fall, creating a physiological state that mimics starvation. This hormonal shift helps explain why sleep-deprived individuals tend to crave calorie-dense, carbohydrate-rich foods. One controlled laboratory study found that participants restricted to four hours of sleep consumed an average of 300 additional calories the following day compared to when they slept eight hours.

Recognizing this cycle presents an opportunity. Improving diet and sleep simultaneously — rather than treating them as separate goals — may offer a more effective path to better overall health. Simple strategies include establishing a consistent bedtime, avoiding caffeine after early afternoon, and choosing a light evening snack that combines complex carbohydrates with a small amount of protein, such as whole-grain crackers with cheese or a banana with almond butter. These small adjustments can help break the cycle and promote both better nutrition and more restful nights.

Adapted from nutrition and sleep medicine research literature.

Correct Answer: B. The passage emphasizes the "bidirectional relationship" from the first paragraph onward. A is too extreme and inaccurate; C focuses on one detail; D contradicts the passage's advice about light evening snacks.

According to the passage, participants who ate a high-fiber, low-saturated-fat diet experienced what benefit compared to those eating processed foods?
Correct Answer: C. The second paragraph states: "a 17-minute reduction in the time it took to drift off." The 300-calorie figure comes from a different study about sleep-deprived participants, not the diet study.

Correct Answer: B. The passage pairs ghrelin and leptin, describing one as signaling hunger and the other as signaling "satiety." The contrast with "hunger" indicates satiety means fullness or satisfaction — not hunger, a medical condition, or digestion.
Question C4 — Inference
The passage suggests that a person who consistently sleeps fewer than six hours per night would most likely:
Correct Answer: B. The fourth paragraph directly states: "When a person sleeps fewer than six hours per night, ghrelin levels rise and leptin levels fall." This physiological state increases cravings for calorie-dense foods, making B the correct inference.
Question C5 — Author's Tone
The author's attitude toward the strategy of improving diet and sleep simultaneously is best described as:
Correct Answer: B. The final paragraph uses phrases like "presents an opportunity," "more effective path," and "simple strategies" with concrete suggestions. This tone is encouraging and practical, not doubtful, amused, or outraged.

7. Passage D — Technology & Innovation

The Diffusion of Innovation: Why Some Technologies Catch On

Throughout history, certain inventions have spread rapidly through society while others, seemingly just as clever, have languished in obscurity. The QWERTY keyboard layout, designed in the 1870s to prevent typewriter jams, remains the global standard nearly 150 years later despite the existence of layouts proven to be more efficient. Electric cars were commercially available in the 1890s — decades before the Model T — yet internal combustion engines came to dominate the twentieth century. Why do some innovations succeed while others fail?

In 1962, sociologist Everett Rogers proposed his influential "Diffusion of Innovations" theory to explain this phenomenon. Rogers identified five key attributes that determine how quickly a new idea or technology is adopted. The first is relative advantage: the degree to which an innovation is perceived as better than what it replaces. The second is compatibility: how well the innovation aligns with existing values, past experiences, and current needs of potential adopters. The third is complexity: if an innovation is difficult to understand or use, adoption will be slower. The fourth is trialability: the extent to which an innovation can be tested on a limited basis before commitment. The fifth and final attribute is observability: how visible the results of an innovation are to others.

These five attributes help explain many historical technology adoption patterns. The smartphone spread with astonishing speed because it offered clear relative advantages over traditional cell phones (internet access, apps, GPS), was compatible with existing communication habits, was simple enough for non-technical users to grasp intuitively, could be tried in a store before purchase, and its benefits were highly visible as friends and colleagues demonstrated their devices. Conversely, the Segway personal transporter — hyped before its 2001 launch as a world-changing invention — struggled in part because it was incompatible with existing transportation infrastructure, difficult to categorize legally, and its advantages over walking or cycling were not immediately obvious to most consumers.

Rogers also categorized adopters into five groups based on their willingness to embrace new ideas. Innovators, about 2.5% of the population, are risk-takers who eagerly try new things. Early adopters, roughly 13.5%, are opinion leaders who influence the next group: the early majority (34%). The late majority (another 34%) are skeptical and adopt only after most others have done so. Laggards, the final 16%, are traditionalists who resist change until absolutely necessary.

Understanding these principles has practical value beyond academic theory. Entrepreneurs can design products with adoption attributes in mind. Policymakers can craft public health campaigns that account for how different segments of the population respond to new practices. Even individuals can benefit: recognizing where we fall on the adoption curve can help us make more deliberate choices about which technologies to embrace — and which to let pass by.

Adapted from Rogers, E.M. "Diffusion of Innovations" and business case studies.

8. Passage E — The Mayan New Year

The Mayan New Year

The timing of New Year's Day has changed as cultures and their calendars evolved. The ancient Mayan civilization celebrated their New Year on the day when the noonday sun was directly overhead. In tropical and sub-tropical latitudes, the sun passes directly overhead twice each year — once on its northward journey and once on its southward journey. At Izapa, in the southern Yucatan, the sun passed directly overhead on August 13. At Edzna, further north, it was directly overhead on July 26. The Mayans celebrated their New Year on August 13 prior to 150 AD. After 150 AD they celebrated it on July 26. This shift in dates correlates with the Mayan civilization's center of power moving from the southern Yucatan to the northern Yucatan.

Official IBEW/NJATC ETA sample passage.

Question E1 — Specific Detail Retrieval
According to the passage, the sun at Edzna was directly overhead at noon on:
Correct Answer: C. The passage states that in tropical and sub-tropical latitudes, the sun passes directly overhead twice each year — once northward and once southward. At Edzna, the passage only mentions the July 26 date, but because the sun passes overhead twice per year at any given tropical latitude, it must also be directly overhead on another date. Option C captures this — July 26 and one other date.
Question E2 — Inference
If the Mayans had moved their civilization's center south of Izapa, their New Year would probably have been closest to:
Correct Answer: D. The passage shows that as the center of power moved north (from Izapa to Edzna), the New Year date shifted earlier — from August 13 to July 26. Moving south of Izapa would therefore shift the date later. Since Izapa (August 13) is already further south than Edzna (July 26), a location south of Izapa would have an overhead sun date later than August 13 — making September 15 the closest option.
Question E3 — Inference / Fact vs. Opinion
Based on the passage, which statement is true?
Correct Answer: C. The passage discusses how the Mayans tracked specific dates (August 13, July 26) and celebrated their New Year based on solar observations, which clearly implies they used calendars to mark the passage of time. Option A is not stated (no mention of climate), B is extreme ("all Mayans" — the passage only says the center of power shifted), and D is not mentioned (the passage never says Izapa was destroyed).

9. Timed Practice Tips

With only ~85 seconds per question, time management is critical. Use these tips to build speed without sacrificing accuracy.

  • Practice with a timer. Set a stopwatch for 8 minutes and 30 seconds when working through a passage with 6 questions. This simulates real test pacing. Gradually reduce your time as you improve.
  • Don't get stuck. If you've spent more than 90 seconds on a single question, mark your best guess, note the question number, and move on. Return to it only if time permits at the end.
  • Answer every question. There is no penalty for wrong answers on the IBEW test. Even a blind guess gives you a 25% chance of being right — never leave a bubble empty.
  • Read the questions first for detail-oriented passages (scientific, technical). This primes your brain to spot relevant facts and saves re-reading time.
  • Read the passage first for argument-driven passages (opinion, persuasive). These require understanding the overall argument before you can answer "main idea" or "tone" questions effectively.
  • Use your pencil. On the real test, actively underline key sentences, circle transition words, and jot brief notes in the margins. Active reading improves retention and speeds up answer retrieval.
  • Watch for extreme language. Answer choices containing words like "always," "never," "completely," or "impossible" are rarely correct — especially for inference and tone questions. The IBEW test favors measured, moderate answers.
  • Simulate test conditions. At least twice before your test date, complete a full 36-question, 51-minute timed practice session — ideally in a quiet environment without distractions. This builds mental stamina and reveals your true pacing.
  • Review your mistakes. After each practice session, spend as much time reviewing wrong answers as you spent answering them. Understanding why you got a question wrong is more valuable than getting it right the first time.
  • Build reading stamina. In the weeks leading up to your test, read challenging material for 20–30 minutes daily — editorials, science articles, historical essays. This trains your brain to maintain focus through dense, unfamiliar content.
