Part A: Evaluating Expressions (Q1–Q5)

Question 1
If A = B + 3(4 − C), what is the value of A when B = 2 and C = 5?
Correct: B) −1
Substitute B = 2 and C = 5: A = 2 + 3(4 − 5) = 2 + 3(−1) = 2 − 3 = −1.
Question 2
Simplify: 24 ÷ 6 × 2 + 3² − 5
Correct: B) 12
Exponent first: 3² = 9. Divide/multiply L→R: 24 ÷ 6 = 4, 4 × 2 = 8. Add/subtract: 8 + 9 − 5 = 12.
Question 3
Evaluate: (15 − 3 × 2) + 4² ÷ 8
Correct: A) 11
Innermost first, multiply before subtract: 3×2=6, (15−6)=9. 4²=16, 16÷8=2. 9+2=11.
Question 4
If x = 3 and y = −4, evaluate: 2x² − 3y + xy
Correct: B) 18
2(3)² − 3(−4) + (3)(−4) = 2(9) + 12 − 12 = 18 + 0 = 18.
Question 5
Evaluate: 5[12 − 2(8 ÷ 4 + 1)]
Correct: C) 30
Inside out: 8÷4=2, 2+1=3. 12−2(3)=12−6=6. 5[6]=30.

Question 19
Use FOIL to multiply: (x + 6)(x − 4)

First: x·x=x². Outer: x·(−4)=−4x. Inner: 6·x=6x. Last: 6·(−4)=−24. Sum: x²+2x−24.

Correct: A) 6x² + x − 15

Correct: C) x² + 14x + 49

Passage A: Urban Planning & City Infrastructure

The Evolution of Modern Urban Planning

Urban planning, the discipline concerned with the design and regulation of the use of space within cities, has undergone profound transformations since the Industrial Revolution. In the nineteenth century, rapid urbanization created overcrowded, unsanitary conditions in major cities across Europe and North America. Factories belched smoke into the air, tenement housing crammed families into windowless rooms, and diseases such as cholera and typhus spread rapidly through contaminated water supplies. These conditions prompted the first wave of formal urban planning, epitomized by Baron Haussmann's renovation of Paris between 1853 and 1870, which replaced narrow medieval streets with wide boulevards, parks, and modern sewer systems.

The twentieth century introduced new models of urban design. Ebenezer Howard's "Garden City" concept proposed self-contained communities surrounded by greenbelts, combining the benefits of town and country living. In the United States, the City Beautiful movement emphasized monumental civic buildings, public parks, and grand boulevards intended to inspire civic pride and moral virtue. However, the post-World War II era saw the rise of automobile-centric development patterns that prioritized highways and suburban expansion over dense urban cores, often at the expense of existing neighborhoods, particularly those housing minority communities.

Contemporary urban planning emphasizes sustainability, mixed-use development, and public transit-oriented design. Planners now recognize that walkable neighborhoods with a diverse mix of housing, employment, retail, and recreational spaces promote healthier lifestyles, reduce carbon emissions, and strengthen community bonds. The concept of "smart cities" integrates digital technology into infrastructure management, using sensors and data analytics to optimize traffic flow, energy consumption, and public services. Yet challenges remain: rapid urbanization in the developing world, the need for affordable housing, climate resilience, and the legacy of past planning decisions that entrenched inequality. Modern urban planners must balance competing interests — economic development, environmental protection, social equity, and quality of life — while navigating complex political landscapes and constrained budgets.

Passage B: History of Electricity

The Journey of Electricity: From Ancient Discovery to Modern Grid

The story of electricity spans millennia, from ancient observations of static electricity in amber to the complex alternating current networks that power modern civilization. The ancient Greeks first documented electrical phenomena around 600 BCE when they noticed that rubbing amber against fur attracted lightweight objects such as feathers. The word "electricity" itself derives from the Greek word "elektron," meaning amber. For over two thousand years, this curious phenomenon remained a scientific curiosity with no practical application.

The scientific revolution of the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries transformed our understanding of electricity. In 1600, English physician William Gilbert published "De Magnete," which distinguished between magnetism and static electricity and coined the term "electricus." Benjamin Franklin's famous kite experiment in 1752 demonstrated that lightning was a form of electrical discharge, leading to his invention of the lightning rod. Alessandro Volta created the first true battery, the voltaic pile, in 1800, providing scientists with a reliable source of continuous electrical current for the first time. This breakthrough enabled a cascade of discoveries: Hans Christian Ørsted found that electric current creates magnetic fields, André-Marie Ampère formulated the mathematical laws of electromagnetism, and Michael Faraday discovered electromagnetic induction — the principle behind electric generators and transformers.

The late nineteenth century witnessed the "War of the Currents" — a fierce commercial battle between Thomas Edison's direct current (DC) system and Nikola Tesla's alternating current (AC) system, backed by industrialist George Westinghouse. While DC was simpler and had been the standard for early electrical distribution, it could not be transmitted efficiently over long distances. Tesla's AC system, using transformers to step voltage up for transmission and down for household use, proved far more practical for large-scale power distribution. The decisive moment came in 1896 when Westinghouse's AC system powered Buffalo, New York, from hydroelectric generators at Niagara Falls, 26 miles away. This achievement demonstrated the viability of long-distance power transmission and established AC as the global standard. Today, electricity is fundamental to nearly every aspect of modern life — from lighting and heating to computing and communications — and the challenge of generating it sustainably through renewable sources like solar and wind power represents one of the defining technological transitions of the twenty-first century.

Passage C: Workplace Safety Practices

Building a Culture of Safety in the Electrical Trades

Workplace safety in the electrical trades is not merely a regulatory requirement — it is a fundamental professional responsibility that protects workers from some of the most severe occupational hazards in any industry. Electricians face unique risks, including arc flash incidents that can generate temperatures exceeding 35,000 degrees Fahrenheit, electrocution from contact with energized conductors, falls from elevated work positions, and injuries from repetitive motion or improper lifting techniques. The Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA) establishes baseline safety standards, but truly effective safety programs go far beyond compliance to create an organizational culture in which every worker feels personally responsible for their own safety and the safety of their colleagues.

The hierarchy of hazard controls provides a systematic framework for managing workplace risks. At the most effective level, hazard elimination involves physically removing the danger — for instance, de-energizing circuits before performing maintenance. Substitution replaces a hazardous condition with a safer alternative, such as using battery-powered tools instead of corded ones in wet environments. Engineering controls isolate workers from hazards through physical barriers, ventilation systems, or machine guarding. Administrative controls change how people work through procedures, training, and scheduling, such as implementing a lockout/tagout program that ensures equipment remains de-energized during servicing. Personal protective equipment (PPE), the last line of defense, includes insulated gloves, arc-rated clothing, hard hats, safety glasses, and steel-toed boots. Importantly, PPE protects only the individual worker and depends on proper use and maintenance, which is why higher-level controls are always preferred.

Electrical safety specifically demands rigorous adherence to protocols that may seem tedious but are proven to save lives. A comprehensive lockout/tagout procedure requires workers to identify all energy sources, notify affected personnel, shut down equipment, isolate energy sources, apply locks and tags, verify isolation by testing, and only then begin work. The National Fire Protection Association's NFPA 70E standard provides detailed guidance on arc flash risk assessment, approach boundaries, and appropriate PPE categories. Regular safety meetings, job hazard analyses conducted before each shift, and a non-punitive near-miss reporting system all contribute to continuous improvement in safety outcomes. Ultimately, the most effective safety cultures are those in which workers feel empowered to stop work when they observe an unsafe condition — a principle known as "stop-work authority" — reinforcing that no deadline, budget, or productivity target is worth a preventable injury or fatality.

Passage D: Environmental Conservation

Conservation and the Electrical Industry: Toward a Sustainable Future

Environmental conservation has evolved from a niche concern of naturalists in the nineteenth century to a central imperative of twenty-first-century industrial policy. The electrical industry, paradoxically, stands at both ends of the environmental spectrum: it is simultaneously a major consumer of natural resources and a crucial enabler of the transition to a sustainable, low-carbon economy. Understanding this dual role is essential for the next generation of electrical workers who will be called upon to design, install, and maintain the infrastructure of a greener world.

The environmental impact of traditional electricity generation is well documented. Coal-fired power plants — which as recently as 2010 supplied nearly half of America's electricity — emit carbon dioxide, sulfur dioxide, nitrogen oxides, and particulate matter that contribute to climate change, acid rain, and respiratory illness. The extraction of coal through mountaintop removal mining has devastated ecosystems across Appalachia, while the disposal of coal ash has contaminated groundwater in numerous communities. Natural gas, while burning cleaner than coal, releases methane during extraction, a greenhouse gas far more potent than carbon dioxide over shorter time horizons. Even hydroelectric dams, despite producing emission-free electricity, can disrupt river ecosystems, block fish migration, and displace human communities. This complex web of environmental costs has driven a fundamental rethinking of how we generate power.

The transition to renewable energy sources — primarily solar photovoltaics, wind turbines, and in some regions geothermal and tidal power — represents the most significant transformation of the electrical grid since its original construction. Solar capacity in the United States has grown more than a hundredfold since 2010, while wind power has become cost-competitive with fossil fuels in many markets. Electrical workers are at the forefront of this transition: installing rooftop solar arrays, wiring electric vehicle charging stations, retrofitting buildings with energy-efficient lighting and HVAC systems, and constructing the smart grid infrastructure that will manage a distributed, bidirectional flow of electricity. The concept of "green building" standards, such as LEED certification, has created new specialties within the electrical trades focused on energy auditing, sustainable design, and renewable integration. Far from being a threat to traditional electrical work, the conservation movement has created unprecedented demand for skilled electricians who understand both conventional systems and emerging green technologies.

Passage E: The Mayan New Year

How the Ancient Maya Celebrated the New Year

The ancient Maya civilization of Mesoamerica developed one of the most sophisticated calendar systems in human history. Unlike the modern Gregorian calendar that marks a single New Year's Day on January 1st, the Maya actually celebrated multiple "New Year" days throughout their ritual calendar cycle. The Maya used a complex interlocking system of calendars, the most important being the 260-day Tzolk'in ritual calendar and the 365-day Haab' solar calendar. Every 52 solar years, these two calendars would align, marking a complete Calendar Round — an event of tremendous religious significance that the Maya celebrated with elaborate ceremonies, offerings to the gods, and the ritual extinguishing and relighting of all fires.

The Maya New Year, known as Wayeb', was actually a five-day period at the end of the Haab' year that was considered a dangerous and unlucky time when the boundaries between the mortal world and the supernatural realm were thought to be thin. During Wayeb', the Maya would fast, abstain from normal activities, and perform purification rituals to ward off evil spirits before the New Year began. The New Year's Day itself varied depending on which of the four "Year Bearer" days — Ik', Manik', Eb', or Kaban — fell at the start of the new Haab' cycle, creating a four-year rotation of New Year celebrations each associated with a different cardinal direction, color, and set of patron deities. This rotating system ensured that the Maya maintained a rich and varied ceremonial calendar that bound their complex understanding of time to their spiritual and agricultural lives.
