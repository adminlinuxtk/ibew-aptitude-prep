How to Prepare

The IBEW Aptitude Test is not a test you can cram for the night before. It measures foundational algebra and reading skills that require consistent practice to sharpen. Most successful candidates start preparing 4–6 weeks before their scheduled test date and follow a structured plan.

Important: This test is administered without a calculator. If you rely on a calculator for everyday math, you must begin doing all arithmetic by hand immediately. Work every practice problem without one.

Start Early

Give yourself at least 4–6 weeks. This allows time to review every topic, take practice tests, and revisit weak areas without rushing. Starting earlier (8+ weeks) is even better if math is not your strong suit.

Diagnose First

Before diving into lessons, take a full-length diagnostic practice test. This tells you exactly which topics need the most attention so you don't waste time on material you already know.

Follow a Schedule

Consistency beats intensity. 30–45 minutes of focused practice every day is far more effective than 4-hour cram sessions on weekends. Use the 8-week plan below as your template.

Review Every Mistake

Getting a question wrong is an opportunity. Spend more time understanding why you missed it than celebrating correct answers. Read every explanation carefully and re-work the problem from scratch.

Simulate Test Conditions

Practice with a timer. The algebra section gives you about 1 minute 24 seconds per question and the reading section about 1 minute 25 seconds per question. Build your pacing early.

Build Mental Stamina

The full test lasts 97 minutes with only a short break between sections. In the final weeks, do full-length practice sessions so your focus doesn't fade halfway through.

8-Week Study Schedule

This schedule is designed for someone who has a basic familiarity with algebra but needs to rebuild fluency. Adjust the daily time up or down based on your starting point — the key is consistency.

Week Focus Areas Daily Time Practice Goal
Week 1 PEMDAS (order of operations), evaluating algebraic expressions, fraction and decimal arithmetic 30 min/day 10 problems/day
Week 2 Linear equations (one-step, two-step, multi-step), solving inequalities, basic word problems 45 min/day 15 problems/day
Week 3 Systems of equations (substitution & elimination), polynomial operations (add, subtract, multiply) 45 min/day 15 problems/day
Week 4 Factoring polynomials (GCF, trinomials, grouping), FOIL method, introduction to quadratic equations 45 min/day 15 problems/day
Week 5 Functions (notation, evaluation, domain/range), graphing lines, Cartesian plane, slope and intercepts 45 min/day 15 problems/day
Week 6 Number series and sequences (arithmetic, geometric, alternating), exponent rules, radicals 30 min/day 10 problems + 1 reading passage/day
Week 7 Reading comprehension strategies (main idea, inference, detail, vocabulary in context), timed reading practice 30 min/day 2–3 practice passages/day
Week 8 Full-length timed practice tests, review all weak areas identified in prior weeks, final formula memorization 60 min/day 2 full practice tests
Pro Tip: On "light" days (30 minutes), use the extra time to review flashcards of formulas or re-work problems you got wrong earlier in the week. Spaced repetition is one of the most powerful learning techniques.

Test-Day Tips

What you do in the 24 hours before and the morning of the test can have a significant impact on your score. Follow these guidelines to show up at your best.

The Night Before: Sleep

Aim for 7–9 hours of quality sleep. Avoid screens for at least 30 minutes before bed. Do a light review of your formula sheet but do not attempt new or difficult problems — save your mental energy. Set two alarms and lay out your clothes, ID, and directions the night before.

The Morning Of: Eat

Eat a balanced breakfast with protein, complex carbs, and healthy fats — think eggs and whole-grain toast, oatmeal with nuts, or yogurt with fruit. Avoid sugary cereals or pastries that cause an energy crash. Drink water but don't overdo it; you don't want to need a bathroom break mid-section. Limit caffeine to your normal amount.

Arrive Early

Plan to arrive at least 30 minutes before your scheduled test time. This gives you a buffer for traffic, parking, and finding the testing room. Rushing in at the last minute spikes your stress hormones and hurts performance. Use the extra time to breathe, use the restroom, and settle in.

What to Bring

Bring a valid government-issued photo ID (driver's license, passport, state ID). The test center provides pencils and scratch paper. Calculators, phones, smartwatches, and personal scratch paper are NOT allowed — leave them in your car or at home. Bring a light jacket in case the room is cold. Some test centers allow a clear water bottle; check with your local JATC beforehand.

Mindset

Remind yourself that you have prepared for this. If you hit a hard question, skip it and come back — don't let one tough problem eat up time you need for five easier ones. You cannot return to the algebra section once the reading section begins, so pace yourself carefully. The test is designed to measure aptitude, not perfection; a passing score of 4 out of 9 is achievable with solid preparation. Breathe, trust your training, and work steadily.

Formula Cheat Sheet

These are the formulas and rules you must have memorized before test day. There is no formula sheet provided during the actual exam. Review these daily, write them out from memory, and apply them in practice problems until they become automatic.

Order of Operations — PEMDAS
P — Parentheses first
E — Exponents (powers, roots)
MD — Multiplication & Division (left → right)
AS — Addition & Subtraction (left → right)
Slope-Intercept Form
y = mx + b
Where: m = slope, b = y-intercept
Slope Formula (from two points)
m = (y₂ − y₁) / (x₂ − x₁)
Rise over run: change in y divided by change in x
Point-Slope Form
y − y₁ = m(x − x₁)
Used when you know one point (x₁, y₁) and the slope m
Difference of Squares
a² − b² = (a + b)(a − b)
Example: x² − 25 = (x + 5)(x − 5)
FOIL Method
(a + b)(c + d) = ac + ad + bc + bd
First, Outer, Inner, Last — then combine like terms
Quadratic Factoring (a = 1)
x² + Bx + C = (x + p)(x + q)
Where p + q = B and p · q = C
Quadratic Formula
x = [−b ± √(b² − 4ac)] / 2a
For any quadratic: ax² + bx + c = 0
Distributive Property
a(b + c) = ab + ac
Also: a(b − c) = ab − ac
Exponent Rules
Product: xᵃ · xᵇ = xᵃ⁺ᵇ
Quotient: xᵃ / xᵇ = xᵃ⁻ᵇ
Power: (xᵃ)ᵇ = xᵃᵇ
Zero: x⁰ = 1 (x ≠ 0)
Negative: x⁻ᵃ = 1 / xᵃ
Build Slope-Intercept from Two Points
Step 1: m = (y₂ − y₁) / (x₂ − x₁)
Step 2: Plug m and one point into y = mx + b to solve for b
Step 3: Write final equation: y = mx + b
Memorization Tip: Write these formulas on index cards — the formula name on one side, the formula itself on the other. Review the deck for 5 minutes every morning. When you can write every formula from memory on a blank sheet of paper without hesitation, you're ready.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Even well-prepared candidates lose points to these predictable errors. Learn them now so you can spot and sidestep them on test day.

1 Forgetting PEMDAS Order

The most frequent mistake: adding before multiplying, or working right-to-left instead of left-to-right for equal-priority operations. Remember: multiplication and division are equal priority (work left to right), as are addition and subtraction. When in doubt, use parentheses to make the order explicit in your scratch work.

2 Sign Errors with Negative Numbers

Subtracting a negative is adding: 5 − (−3) = 8, not 2. When substituting negative values into expressions, always wrap the negative number in parentheses: 2x² with x = −3 becomes 2(−3)² = 2(9) = 18, not 2(−3²) = 2(−9) = −18.

3 Misapplying the Distributive Property

Every term inside parentheses must be multiplied by the factor outside: 3(x + 4) = 3x + 12, not 3x + 4. The same applies with subtraction: −2(x − 5) = −2x + 10, not −2x − 10. The negative sign distributes to all terms inside.

4 Dropping the Negative When Moving Terms

When you move a term from one side of an equation to the other, its sign flips: 3x + 5 = 2x − 7 becomes 3x − 2x = −7 − 5, which gives x = −12. A common error is writing −7 + 5 instead of −7 − 5.

5 Misreading the Slope Formula

The slope formula is (y₂ − y₁) / (x₂ − x₁), not (x₂ − x₁) / (y₂ − y₁). Mixing up the numerator and denominator is surprisingly common under time pressure. Double-check: slope is rise over run (y over x).

6 Not Checking the Answer

You can verify nearly every algebra answer by plugging it back into the original equation. This takes 15–20 seconds and can catch sign errors, arithmetic mistakes, and misread problems. On the actual test, use this technique on any question where you have extra time.

7 Spending Too Long on One Question

With roughly 1 minute 24 seconds per algebra question, getting stuck on one problem for 3+ minutes is a strategic mistake. Every question is worth the same amount. If you're not making progress after 60–90 seconds, mark it, skip it, and return if time permits. Protect your opportunity to answer the easier questions that follow.

8 Skipping Reading Passage Strategies

Many candidates focus entirely on math and neglect reading comprehension. The reading section is 36 questions in 51 minutes — it counts just as much as the math section. Skim the questions before reading the passage so you know what to look for. Read actively, underline key points mentally, and avoid the trap of choosing an answer that "sounds right" but isn't supported by the passage.

Mechanical Concepts (Some Locals)

Some IBEW locals (including Local 134 in Chicago) include a mechanical comprehension section on the aptitude test. These questions test basic physics and mechanical principles. While not on every version of the test, it's smart to understand the fundamentals.

Pulley Systems

Pulleys reduce the force needed to lift a load. The more lines/ropes supporting the load, the less tension on each rope.

  • Single fixed pulley: Changes direction only — same force required
  • Multiple pulley system: More lines = less force per line
  • Balanced load rule: The weight must be centered. Uneven loads create tipping hazards
  • Key formula: Force needed = Load weight ÷ Number of supporting ropes (ideal, ignoring friction)

Levers

A lever uses a fulcrum to multiply force. The distance from fulcrum determines mechanical advantage.

  • First-class lever: Fulcrum in the middle (seesaw, crowbar)
  • Second-class lever: Load in the middle (wheelbarrow)
  • Third-class lever: Effort in the middle (fishing rod, tweezers)
  • Law of the lever: Effort × Effort arm = Load × Load arm

Gears

Gears transfer rotational motion and change speed/torque between shafts.

  • Driving gear (input) turns the driven gear (output)
  • Adjacent gears spin in opposite directions
  • Gear ratio: Number of teeth on driven gear ÷ teeth on driving gear
  • Speed vs. torque trade-off: Larger gear = slower speed, more torque. Smaller gear = faster speed, less torque

Load Distribution

Understanding how weight distributes is critical in electrical work (conduit supports, panel mounting, etc.).

  • Center of gravity: The point where weight is evenly balanced in all directions
  • More support points = less weight per support (but requires even distribution)
  • Triangulation: Triangular bracing distributes loads and prevents racking — the strongest basic structural shape

What Happens After the Test

The aptitude test is just the first step in the journey toward becoming an IBEW electrician apprentice. Here's what to expect once you put your pencil down.

Scoring & Timeline

Your test is scored on a stanine scale from 1 to 9. A score of 4 or higher is typically considered passing, though individual JATCs (Joint Apprenticeship and Training Committees) set their own cutoffs — some require a 5. Results are usually available within 1–2 weeks after your test date. You will be notified by mail or email with your score and next steps.

4–9
Stanine Score Range — 4 is typical minimum

The Interview

If you meet or exceed the passing score, you will be invited to an oral interview with representatives from the local JATC — typically a panel of 4–6 people including union representatives and contractor representatives. The interview assesses your:

  • Interest in and knowledge of the electrical trade
  • Work ethic, reliability, and attitude
  • Previous experience (construction, mechanical, or technical work)
  • Ability to complete the 5-year apprenticeship program
  • Communication and problem-solving skills

Dress professionally (business casual minimum), arrive early, bring copies of your résumé and any relevant certifications, and prepare answers to common interview questions. The interview typically lasts 15–30 minutes.

Ranking & The Eligibility List

After your interview, you receive a composite score that combines your aptitude test results with your interview performance. You are then placed on an eligibility list (ranking list) for your local JATC. Your rank determines when you are called for apprenticeship placement. Candidates with the highest composite scores are called first. Rankings are typically valid for 2 years, after which you may need to reapply and retest.

Be patient: Depending on your local's demand for apprentices, you may wait anywhere from a few weeks to over a year after being ranked. During this time, gaining related experience (construction, laborer, material handler) can improve your standing with some locals.

The Apprenticeship

Once selected from the eligibility list, you begin a 5-year apprenticeship program combining:

  • On-the-Job Training (OJT): 8,000 hours of paid, hands-on electrical work under the supervision of a licensed journeyman electrician. You earn a progressive wage that increases as you advance through the program.
  • Classroom Instruction: Typically 160–200 hours per year at an approved training center. You study electrical theory, code (NEC), safety, blueprint reading, and specialized systems.
  • Certifications: You'll earn industry certifications throughout the program, culminating in eligibility to take your state's journeyman electrician licensing exam.

Upon completion, you graduate as a Journeyman Inside Wireman — one of the most respected and well-compensated trades in the construction industry, with strong union benefits, a pension, and continuing education opportunities throughout your career.

What If You Don't Pass?

If your score is below the cutoff, you must wait 3 months (90 days) before you can retake the test. Use this time to:

  • Identify exactly which topics gave you trouble
  • Work through focused lessons and additional practice problems
  • Take multiple full-length practice tests under timed conditions
  • Consider a prep course (Electric Prep, iPREP) for structured guidance

Many successful electricians did not pass on their first attempt. A retake is not a failure — it's a second opportunity to demonstrate your readiness. Approach it with a stronger preparation plan and you can significantly improve your score.
